About the Author

Vasily Akimovich Nikiforov-Volgin
1900 – 1941

☩

Vasily Akimovich Nikiforov-Volgin, a unique Orthodox Christian writer, was born on December 24, 1900 (January 6, 1901, New Style) in the village of Markushi, Kalyazinsky Uyezd, in the Tver Province, on the Volga River, into a deeply religious peasant family. His father was a cobbler. Soon after his birth, the Nikiforov family moved to Narva (which was then part of the St. Petersburg Governorate).

After 1920, according to the Treaty of Tartu, the territory on the right bank of the Narova River (Narva with the Prinarovye region) was transferred to the Republic of Estonia. Consequently, the writer's family became involuntary emigrants, cut off from their dearly beloved Russian homeland. However, in the 1920s and 30s, the old Russian way of life, with its customs and traditions, order and practices, was still preserved there. It was this life that Nikiforov-Volgin described in his sketches and stories.

The future writer only managed to complete his studies at the parish school of the St. Vladimir Brotherhood. However, it was a good school. The young man engaged in serious self-education extensively. His passion for books and reading manifested itself in early childhood. Deeply religious from childhood and possessing a high-pitched voice with clear, expressive diction, he became a precentor* at the Holy Transfiguration Cathedral in Narva and remained one until the spring of 1932. This is the source of his excellent knowledge of church life and the clergy's way of life, which is evident in his works.

According to the recollections of one of his friends, Vasily loved to take a notebook and pencil and sneak into a secluded corner of the church watchhouse before the service began, listening to worshippers and people who just came in to warm up, and writing down what they were talking about among themselves. Their poignant remarks and folk expressions later found their way into his stories.

In 1923, he began his regular literary and journalistic activities. In 1927, he received his first award for writing: at a competition for young authors organized by the oldest Estonian literary association — The Literary Circle in Tallinn — first prize was awarded to V. Nikiforov-Volgin's story, "Bowing to the Ground." He usually signed his publications with the pseudonym Vasily Volgin — in memory of the great Russian river on which his native village was located. Over time, he began signing his name as V. Nikiforov-Volgin, combining his surname with his pseudonym.

By the mid-1930s, Nikiforov-Volgin was already known in the literary circles of the Russian Diaspora, corresponding with I. Shmelev, A. Amfiteatrov, B. Zaitsev, and others. In early 1936, he moved to Tallinn, becoming a home tutor and educator. Among his pupils was Alexy (Ridiger), the future Patriarch of Moscow and All Russia, Alexy II.

Two collections of his stories were published — "The Earth Celebrates Her Name Day" (1937) and "The Pilgrim's Staff" (1938), which were met with positive reviews in Russian publications abroad.

According to the recollections of those who knew him, Vasily Nikiforov was an exceptionally kind, warm-hearted, and responsive person. His sisters told how once, in severe frost, Vasily gave his hat and winter coat to a beggar on the street, justifying himself at home by saying: "I felt sorry for the man — he was freezing, and I'll manage somehow…"

Nikiforov-Volgin was a deeply religious man, and this primarily defined his worldview and the character of all his work. Memoir-stories about childhood constituted a significant part of his work. He depicted scenes of church services, church holidays, and a boy's communication with "holy people." Nikiforov-Volgin depicts this world of the church and believers — so familiar to him — accurately, authentically, and with love. He manages to recreate all the minutiae of this world in his works, including everyday details, and at the same time convey its special atmosphere, its spirit, its hidden beauty.

Regarding his stories, he wrote in a letter to a friend: "There is no fiction in my story. I had to go through and experience all this. The names of the characters are changed. Some of them are no longer with us."

On May 24, 1941, the writer was arrested and transported to the city of Kirov. On December 14, 1941, he was shot for "publishing books, pamphlets, and plays of a slanderous, anti-Soviet nature." He was rehabilitated in 1991.

* "Precentor" is an approximate translation of the Russian term псаломщик (psalomshchik). He would regulate the singing and reading in the choir, coordinate the order of worship, and also functioned as the parish clerk, maintaining all church records and handling the parish's official correspondence.
